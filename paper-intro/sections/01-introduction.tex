\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

When a country plans its energy transition, the plan is drawn by an energy-system
model: a least-cost optimiser that chooses which power plants to build, when to build
them, and how to run them, subject to the engineering of the grid and the policies of
the government. The questions the country's ministers then ask are almost never about
the plan itself. They are about the economy around it. What will the plan do to
electricity prices, and through them to wages and the cost of living? Who bears the
burden---and is it the poor, for whom electricity is a larger share of the budget? What
does the build-out do to the public debt? How many lives does cleaner air save, and are
the people it saves workers or retirees? None of these questions can be answered by the
model that produced the plan, because that model contains no households, no firms, no
government budget, and no prices that anyone responds to. Demand is a fixed input;
``cost'' is a total, silent about who pays it.

The tools that could answer those questions---general-equilibrium models of the
macroeconomy---have the opposite blindness. They carry behaviour, taxes, and
distribution, but no energy technology: no dispatch, no capacity constraints, no fuel
mix, no emissions accounting worth the name. The economics of the energy transition
therefore sits in an awkward seam between two mature modelling traditions, each blind
precisely where the other sees. This seam is not new---it has been worked since the oil
shocks of the 1970s---but the questions now crossing it are more consequential than
they have ever been: carbon pricing is spreading, national climate pledges must be
costed, development finance is conditioned on credible transition plans, and the
distributional politics of energy prices can decide whether a reform survives contact
with an electorate.

This paper introduces a way across the seam, built from two open-source models that
countries already use, and shows what crossing it buys. On the energy side is
\CLEWS{}---Climate, Land, Energy and Water Systems \citep{howells_clews_2013}---a
least-cost planning framework built on the \OSeMOSYS{} optimiser
\citep{howells_osemosys_2011} and deployed with national teams so that the country owns
its own model. On the economy side is \OGCore{} \citep{debacker_evans_ogcore}, an
overlapping-generations (OLG) general-equilibrium platform in the
\citet{auerbach_kotlikoff_1987} tradition, with households differentiated by age and
lifetime income, multiple industries, and a government that must balance its budget
with real tax instruments. The two are joined by a \emph{channel-based soft link}: the
solved energy scenario is read for a small set of economically meaningful
signals---the electricity price, the required grid investment, the emissions
path---and each signal is carried into the economy by exactly one model parameter it
can legitimately move, with the economy's equilibrium return and activity sent back.
Every channel is a named, auditable economic mechanism; no signal is counted twice. A
companion paper \citep{lafleur_companion} develops the framework formally and
validates each mechanism; this paper explains why the link exists, what it adds to the
existing ways of joining energy and macro models, and what it looks like in use.

What it looks like in use is best shown by example. We couple the Philippine \CLEWS{}
case---a baseline against an announced energy-policy reform---to a Philippine
multi-industry calibration of \OGCore{}. The reform raises the levelized cost of
electricity by about $5\%$ on average (peaking near $16\%$ around 2030), and the link
transmits that real price, not a stylised shock, through industry costs and household
budgets. Three findings illustrate what the coupled tool can say that neither parent
could. First, the macroeconomic effect of the reform \emph{changes sign with the
horizon}: output rises roughly $+0.2\%$ over the first decade of the transition but
settles $-0.14\%$ below baseline in the long run. The near-term gain is a health
effect---cleaner air makes the working-age population measurably more
productive---while the long-run cost combines higher energy prices with, perversely
only at first glance, the demographic arithmetic of survival: the lives cleaner air
saves are concentrated among the elderly, so the steady state has more retirees per
worker. A static or representative-agent model cannot even express this horizon
structure. Second, the health effect itself is disciplined: the reform's $2.7\%$ cut
in power-sector particulate emissions saves about $95$ lives per year once a
calibrated dose-response is applied---not the $1{,}165$ a naive reading of the burden
data would suggest. Third, incidence is regressive even when the energy-price revenue
is recycled back to households: aggregate consumption falls about $0.09\%$, and the
burden concentrates on poorer households, for whom electricity is closer to a
necessity. Who pays is an output of the model, not an assumption.

The claim is not that linking energy and macro models is new. It is one of the oldest
projects in energy economics, and Section~\ref{sec:landscape} reviews it: the
hard-linked optimisers that fold a stylised Ramsey economy into the energy program
(MARKAL-MACRO, MESSAGE-MACRO, MERGE, TIMES
\citealp{manne_wene_1992,messner_schrattenholzer_2000,manne_merge_1995,loulou_times_2008});
the hybrid computable-general-equilibrium (CGE) tradition that integrates bottom-up
technology into a top-down economy \citep{bohringer_rutherford_2008,hourcade_hybrid_2006};
the soft-linked national studies that iterate an energy model against a CGE partner
\citep{krook_riekkola_2017,helgesen_tomasgard_2018}; and the integrated assessment
models descending from \citet{nordhaus_dice_2008}. Each of these settles for a macro
side without age structure: a representative agent, or households distinguished by
income but not by cohort, usually without forward-looking life-cycle saving and
usually without a fiscal sector that must actually close. That is precisely the
wrong simplification for today's questions. Distribution across ages and generations
is where energy-transition politics lives \citep{kotlikoff_2021}; health co-benefits
are inherently demographic (they change who is alive, at which ages, with what
productivity); and fiscal closure is where carbon revenue, grid investment, and debt
meet. The OLG tradition holds exactly these pieces. To our knowledge, this link is
the first to join a full overlapping-generations economy to a bottom-up
energy-water-land systems model, and to do so through named channels rather than a
merged optimisation.

Two design choices matter for credibility, and we flag them at the outset. The link is
\emph{soft} and \emph{staged}: the models remain separate programs, exchanging a few
auditable files, because no simultaneous optimisation exists that contains them
both---\OGCore{} traces a transition period by period while \OSeMOSYS{} plans all
years at once, and \OGCore{} exposes no scalar welfare objective an energy LP could
sit inside. And the link is \emph{honestly scoped}: one full \CLEWS{}$\to$\OGCore{}
pass is implemented and validated against a committed regression record; the reverse
signals (the economy's equilibrium return, its energy demand) are emitted and
ready, but the outer fixed-point iteration is designed rather than closed, and the
conversion between \CLEWS{} money and the \OGCore{} numéraire is a stated unit bridge
awaiting calibration. Magnitudes that ride on that bridge are labelled illustrative
wherever they appear.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:landscape} reviews how
energy-macro integration has been done and what each approach cannot answer.
Section~\ref{sec:two-models} introduces the two models and argues that this particular
pair---open-source, country-owned, and structurally complementary---is the right one
to join. Section~\ref{sec:linkages} presents the linkages themselves: the
signal-wedge-channel vocabulary, the electricity-price signal, and the eight channels.
Section~\ref{sec:illustration} works through the Philippine coupling.
Section~\ref{sec:policy} discusses what the linked tool offers policy analysis, and
what it does not yet. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
