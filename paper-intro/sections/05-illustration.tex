\section{The Coupling in Use: The Philippines}
\label{sec:illustration}

We illustrate with the Philippine coupling: the country's \CLEWS{} case (a baseline
against an announced energy-policy reform) joined to the Philippine multi-industry
calibration of \OGCore{} ($M=8$ industries, $I=5$ consumption goods, electricity its
own industry and good). Everything the link consumes is the country's own data: the
electricity price is the reform/baseline levelized-cost ratio from the solved
\CLEWS{} runs---about $1.05$ on average, peaking near $1.16$ around 2030---and the
health inputs are the Philippine ambient-PM$_{2.5}$ burden from the Global Burden of
Disease ($43{,}951$ attributable deaths, with real deaths-by-age and
disability-by-age profiles). The numbers below are the committed regression record
of the coupled run; the exercise demonstrates the machinery on real inputs rather
than evaluating the reform.

The coupled reform---real electricity price, grid investment, a
\$50/tCO\textsubscript{2} carbon price, and the health channel, applied
together---raises output by about $+0.18\%$ on impact and $+0.20\%$ a decade in, but
lowers it $-0.14\%$ in the long-run steady state
(Table~\ref{tab:coupled-intro}, \Cref{fig:transition-intro}). The transition gain is
the health channel at work: the reform's $2.7\%$ cut in power-sector particulate
emissions makes the working-age population healthier and measurably more productive,
and that morbidity gain dominates the early decades. The long-run reversal has two
parts: the contractionary price channels compound, and---the distinctly demographic
part---the roughly $95$ lives per year the cleaner air saves
(\cref{eq:health}: $43{,}951 \times 0.082 \times -0.0265$) are concentrated among
the elderly, so the eventual steady state supports more retirees per worker. A
policy debate armed only with one number---the steady state, or the impact
effect---would reach opposite conclusions; the horizon structure \emph{is} the
finding, and it is invisible to any tool in
Sections~\ref{sec:hard-links}--\ref{sec:iams}.

\subsection{Reading the channels one at a time}
\label{sec:channels-alone}

Run standalone, each channel behaves as its mechanism predicts, and all are small:
the energy price alone moves steady-state output $-0.006\%$ (a real $5\%$ price
rise, honestly transmitted, is simply not a large macro shock---a discipline lost
when stylised $+20\%$ shocks stand in for data); carbon is the largest single
contraction at $-0.021\%$; capital intensity barely moves the aggregate while
reshaping the energy sector; health is $-0.0003\%$ at the steady state but $+0.19\%$
near-term. Their sum ($\approx-0.02\%$) is an order of magnitude short of the
coupled steady state ($-0.138\%$): the channels interact in general equilibrium, so
the one-at-a-time table is a diagnostic reading, not an additive accounting---itself
a caution against the common practice of costing policy packages channel by channel.

\subsection{Who pays}
\label{sec:who-pays}

Incidence is where the OLG side earns its keep. Under the recycled electricity-price
experiment---the price change applied to households with all revenue returned as
transfers---aggregate consumption still falls ($-0.092\%$ at the steady state), and
the burden concentrates on lower-income households, for whom electricity sits close
to the subsistence floor of the demand system. Recycling softens but does not undo
regressivity; that is a quantitative answer, by age and income group, to the
question ministers actually ask, produced by the same tool that carries the energy
plan.

\caveat{The energy-price and health inputs are real (a \CLEWS{} price ratio and the
GBD burden); the carbon and investment magnitudes ride on the uncalibrated
money-to-numéraire bridge and are illustrative. Mechanics are validated throughout;
levels are labelled.}
