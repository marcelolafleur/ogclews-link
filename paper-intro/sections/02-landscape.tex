\section{How Energy and Macroeconomic Models Have Been Linked}
\label{sec:landscape}

The integration of energy-system and macroeconomic models has been pursued since the
oil shocks of the 1970s, and the approaches fall into four families. They differ
along two dimensions: how much economic structure sits beside the energy technology,
and how tightly the two are bound. We review each family against a common test---can
it identify who bears the costs and benefits of an energy policy?---because that
test motivates the framework this paper introduces.

\subsection{Hard links}
\label{sec:hard-links}

The oldest and tightest approach embeds a small macroeconomy directly in the energy
optimisation, so that a single program solves both. MARKAL-MACRO
\citep{manne_wene_1992} attached a one-sector Ramsey growth model to the MARKAL
energy system; MESSAGE-MACRO \citep{messner_schrattenholzer_2000} links the MESSAGE
model to a macroeconomic module and iterates the pair to consistency; MERGE
\citep{manne_merge_1995} and the TIMES family \citep{loulou_times_2008} carry the
design forward. The gain is substantive: energy demand responds to price within the
solution, so the cost of a constraint is evaluated against an economy that adjusts.

The cost of the tightness is the economy itself. For the macroeconomic side to enter
the optimisation, it must be reduced to a single intertemporal utility
function---one representative agent, one aggregate good, saving governed by one
Euler equation. There are no ages, no income groups, no industries, no tax
instruments, and no government budget constraint in any operational sense. The
hard-linked family can report the aggregate consumption cost of a carbon constraint;
it cannot report incidence, because no agent in the model can be affected
differently from any other. The approach also has a structural prerequisite that
matters for what follows: hard-linking requires the economy to expose a scalar
objective the energy program can join. An overlapping-generations economy is an
equilibrium among cohorts rather than a planner's optimum and has no such objective,
so the hard-link route is unavailable for precisely the class of models that carries
distribution.

\subsection{Hybrid CGE models}
\label{sec:hybrid-cge}

The converse approach embeds bottom-up technology detail in a top-down computable
general equilibrium model. \citet{bohringer_rutherford_2008} provide the
methodological foundation, formulating discrete energy technologies and a full
general equilibrium economy as one mixed complementarity problem;
\citet{hourcade_hybrid_2006} frame the broader hybrid-modelling agenda. Applied
models in this tradition range from global trade-and-energy CGEs to national policy
models \citep[e.g.][]{goulder_hafstead_2017}, and the tradition has produced serious
distributional work: \citet{rausch_2011} attach household micro-data to a CGE model
and estimate the incidence of carbon pricing across income groups.

Two limitations persist. First, the economy is typically recursive-dynamic: it rolls
forward with a fixed saving rule rather than solving forward-looking life-cycle
behaviour, so questions about anticipation, cohort redistribution, and transition
timing are outside its scope. Second, households may be heterogeneous by income but
not by age: there are no cohorts, no retirement, and no demography, so the model
cannot express a mechanism in which cleaner air changes the size and age composition
of the population. Hybrid CGE models can estimate incidence across the income
distribution within a period; they cannot estimate incidence across generations, and
they cannot carry a demographic health channel.

\subsection{Soft links}
\label{sec:soft-links}

Between the two poles, soft-linked studies keep both models intact, pass solved
results between them, and iterate toward consistency. \citet{wene_1996} formalises
the approach and its convergence logic; \citet{krook_riekkola_2017} document the
practical challenges in a Swedish TIMES-CGE application; and
\citet{helgesen_tomasgard_2018} compare linking with full integration and study the
effects on equilibria and convergence. The soft link trades formal elegance for
transparency: each model retains its full structure and solution concept, and the
exchange between them is explicit and inspectable.

The limitation in this literature is the macroeconomic partner rather than the
method: the macro side has been a representative-agent or income-class CGE, with the
limitations noted above. The method itself is the appropriate one for a
macroeconomic model that cannot be hard-linked---which, as noted, an OLG economy
structurally cannot. The framework in this paper is a soft link in the tradition of
\citet{wene_1996}, with a different class of model on the economy side.

\subsection{Integrated assessment models}
\label{sec:iams}

Integrated assessment models address a different question---optimal global
abatement, the social cost of carbon---and make correspondingly different
simplifications. The DICE lineage \citep{nordhaus_dice_2008} reduces both sides:
energy technology to an aggregate abatement-cost function and the economy to a
global Ramsey planner. Process-based IAMs restore technological detail but retain
the aggregate economy. The family's best-known controversy is instructive here: the
disagreement between \citet{stern_2007} and \citet{nordhaus_stern_2007} turned on a
discount rate that must be assumed, because a one-agent model contains no market to
reveal one. An economy with an equilibrium interest rate can instead supply the
discount rate to the energy planner, and the framework below does so
(Section~\ref{sec:feedback-channels}).

\subsection{The remaining gap}
\label{sec:open-cell}

Arranging the four families along the two dimensions shows which combinations exist.
Rich technology beside a rich aggregate economy exists (hybrid CGE); rich technology
beside a thin optimising economy exists (hard links, process IAMs); and OLG
economies with full fiscal and demographic structure exist without energy
technology---\citet{kotlikoff_2021} analyse carbon taxation in an OLG setting
precisely to measure generational incidence, with climate entering through an
aggregate damage function rather than a planned energy system. The combination that
does not exist is bottom-up energy-water-land technology joined to an
overlapping-generations economy with fiscal closure. Supplying it requires a soft
link, since the OLG side rules out hard-linking; a disciplined interface, so that
the flexibility of a soft link does not become arbitrariness; and a pair of models
that countries can obtain, run, and audit. The next two sections address these in
turn.
