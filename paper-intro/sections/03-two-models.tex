\section{The Two Models, and Why This Pair}
\label{sec:two-models}

\subsection{CLEWS: the plan}
\label{sec:clews-intro}

\CLEWS{} \citep{howells_clews_2013} is an integrated climate-land-energy-water
planning framework built on \OSeMOSYS{} \citep{howells_osemosys_2011}, an open-source
linear program that chooses capacity additions and dispatch to meet exogenous
energy-service demands at least discounted system cost, subject to engineering and
policy constraints---capacity adequacy, fuel availability, emissions caps or
penalties, and the intra-annual load shape. Its outputs are the objects planners
argue about: the technology mix, the build schedule, fuel use, emissions, and costs.

Two properties make it the right energy-side partner. First, it is
\emph{institutionally deployed}: \CLEWS{} models are developed with and handed to
national teams, so the energy scenario a country couples to its economy is the
country's own plan, not a stylised import. Second, its gaps are exactly the
complementary ones: demand is a fixed input (no price response), and there are no
households, no government budget, and no distribution---``system cost'' is a total
with no one attached to it.

\subsection{OG-Core: the people}
\label{sec:og-intro}

\OGCore{} \citep{debacker_evans_ogcore} is an open-source overlapping-generations
general-equilibrium platform in the tradition of \citet{auerbach_kotlikoff_1987}.
Households live through $S$ ages in $J$ lifetime-income groups, working, saving, and
consuming $I$ goods with a subsistence floor on necessities; $M$ industries hire
labour and capital; a government levies consumption, business, and income taxes,
issues debt, pays transfers, and invests in public capital---and its budget must
close. The model solves both a long-run steady state and the full transition path to
it, so ``near-term'' and ``eventually'' are different, computable answers. Countries
calibrate it to their own accounts; the Philippine calibration used below has eight
industries and five consumption goods, with electricity isolated as its own industry
and good.

\OGCore{}'s blindness mirrors \CLEWS{}'s sight: firms use capital and labour but no
priced energy input, and there is no technology detail---no plants, no dispatch, no
emissions. What it uniquely holds, among the macro traditions of
Section~\ref{sec:landscape}, is the combination of forward-looking life-cycle
behaviour, age-and-income heterogeneity, demography that policy can move, and a
fiscal sector with real instruments.

\subsection{Why these two, and why a soft link}
\label{sec:why-pair}

The pairing is deliberate on three grounds. \emph{Structural complementarity}: each
model's gap is the other's core competence, with almost no overlap to reconcile---the
usual hybrid-modelling problem of double-counted behaviour barely arises.
\emph{Institutional fit}: both are open source, documented, and built for country
ownership, so a coupled analysis can be run, audited, and re-run by the country it
describes---a requirement for policy work, not a convenience. \emph{Solution-concept
honesty}: the two cannot be merged even in principle. \OGCore{} steps through the
transition period by period as an equilibrium among cohorts; \OSeMOSYS{} optimises
all years at once for a planner; and an OLG economy exposes no scalar welfare
objective an LP could join (Section~\ref{sec:hard-links}). A staged soft link---solve
the energy plan, carry a few signals into the economy, solve the economy, send its
return and demand back---is therefore not a compromise but the correct object. What
a soft link owes its users is discipline at the seam, which is the subject of the
next section.
