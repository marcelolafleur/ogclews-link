\section{The Linkages}
\label{sec:linkages}

The interface between the two models is a small vocabulary applied with discipline.
A \textbf{signal} is a quantity read from a \emph{solved} \CLEWS{} scenario: the
electricity price, the grid investment the build-out requires, the emissions path. A
\textbf{wedge} is the single \OGCore{} parameter that carries a signal into the
economy: a consumption-tax wedge, an industry productivity, the public-investment
share, a survival rate. A \textbf{channel} is one signal mapped to one wedge, with a
diagnostic guard that trips when the mapping is too large or ill-posed for the
reduced form to be trusted. Three rules give the interface its audit trail: every
signal enters through exactly one channel (nothing is counted twice); every channel
is a named economic mechanism with stated assumptions (nothing enters as an opaque
adjustment); and channels that cannot fire honestly---because the country's data
cannot support them---skip loudly rather than improvise. The companion paper
\citep{lafleur_companion} states these as formal principles; here we present the
channels themselves. Table~\ref{tab:channels-glance} lists all eight.

\subsection{The price channels: what the transition costs, and to whom}
\label{sec:price-channels}

The load-bearing signal is the \textbf{electricity price}, taken as the levelized
cost of electricity: the all-in average cost---capital recovery, operation and
maintenance, fuel---of a delivered unit, reconstructed from the solved system's costs
and generation, and passed as a reform/baseline ratio so uncalibrated money units
cancel. (The theoretically cleaner marginal cost, the shadow price on the program's
supply-demand balance, turns out to be degenerate in practice: it binds only in
scattered years, so it is reserved for scarcity analyses.) Handing this price to the
economy buys the two things the energy model cannot do: quantities respond to it, and
incidence falls out of it.

Because \OGCore{} firms do not buy energy explicitly, the price travels through the
two places electricity appears in the country's own accounts. Industries buy most of
it as an input (about $73\%$ of use in the Philippine social accounting matrix), so
each industry's productivity is docked by electricity's share of its input costs---a
cost-push that carries the output-and-wages side of the shock. Households buy the
rest directly (about $25\%$), so the energy good acquires a consumption wedge scaled
by electricity's value share $\sigma$ of that good:
\begin{equation}
  1+\tau^{c,r}_{i_e} \;=\; \bigl(1+\sigma(g-1)\bigr)\bigl(1+\tau^{c,b}_{i_e}\bigr),
  \label{eq:wedge}
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the price ratio and $i_e$ the energy good---the cost-of-living side,
regressive because energy is a partial necessity \citep{stone_1954}. The two pieces
are one composite channel; the electricity industry's own electricity use is netted
out so nothing is double-counted. The \textbf{carbon price} rides the same
infrastructure as a policy lever applied once to both models: an emissions penalty
that tilts the \CLEWS{} build, and an add-on to the household energy wedge on the
macro side---deliberately \emph{not} also inferred from the energy model's implied
carbon cost, which would price the same policy twice.

\subsection{The capital channels: what the build-out does to investment}
\label{sec:capital-channels}

Three channels carry the transition's capital story, and keeping them separate is
the point. \textbf{Public investment} adds the grid's build cost to the
public-investment share, accumulating into public capital: this is where
crowding-out lives, if the spending is debt-financed. \textbf{Capital intensity}
carries the fact that a clean fleet is capital-heavy into the energy industry's
capital \emph{share}---and, counterintuitively, a higher capital share can shed
capital: it lowers the sector's unit cost, the electricity price falls, revenue
falls, and capital follows revenue. The \textbf{investment incentive} (a tax credit)
is the lever that actually pulls capital in, by lowering the sector's cost of
capital. The three act on different objects---public capital, a production exponent,
a user cost---and can move investment in opposite directions; a coupling that merged
them into one ``investment effect'' would erase exactly the distinctions a finance
ministry needs.

\subsection{The people channel: health as demography}
\label{sec:health-channel}

The channel no aggregate model can carry is \textbf{health}. Cleaner power means
lower particulate emissions; the link converts the reform's emissions change into
lives and labour using the country's real Global Burden of Disease data
\citep{gbd_2019_risk} and a calibrated dose-response $M$ that respects two facts:
power is only one source of ambient pollution, and mortality responds less than
proportionally to concentration changes \citep{burnett_gemm_2018}. The lives effect
is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\text{deaths} \;=\; D_{\mathrm{PM}} \times M \times \Delta\text{em},
  \label{eq:health}
\end{equation}
with $D_{\mathrm{PM}}$ the attributable-deaths burden and $\Delta\text{em}$ the
emissions change. What makes this a \emph{demographic} mechanism rather than a
side-calculation is where the result lands: avoided deaths shift the age-specific
survival rates, which changes the population's age structure; morbidity gains scale
the effective-labour profile of the working-age population. Both wedges are
age-specific, which is why the channel's macro signature---a near-term productivity
gain, a long-run rise in retirees per worker---exists at all. It is expressible only
in a model whose households have ages.

\subsection{The feedbacks: what the economy tells the planner}
\label{sec:feedback-channels}

Two channels run the other way, after the economy solves. The \textbf{discount rate}
channel writes \OGCore{}'s equilibrium market return---the first decade's mean of
the household portfolio return---into \CLEWS{}'s planning discount rate, so the
energy planner discounts at the economy's own cost of capital rather than an assumed
social rate; given how much of energy-model output hangs on that one parameter
(Section~\ref{sec:iams}), harmonising it is not a detail. The \textbf{energy demand}
channel scales the demand path \CLEWS{} must meet by the economy's solved activity
change. In a single pass these only stage the next iteration; they are the designed
seam of the loop-closure discussed in Section~\ref{sec:policy}.

\subsection{What the interface does not pretend}
\label{sec:honesty}

Three simplifications are stated rather than hidden. The industry cost-push is
reduced-form: energy is not yet a priced production input, so the input side of the
price shock travels through productivity, calibrated by input shares. The conversion
between \CLEWS{} money and the \OGCore{} numéraire is currently unity, so channels
that depend on it (carbon, investment) have illustrative levels and validated
mechanics. And the coupling runs one full pass; the return signals exist, but the
fixed-point iteration between the models is not yet closed. Each limitation is a
defined seam with a designed successor, not an open flank.
