We develop a framework for quantifying the macroeconomic and distributional effects
of national energy-transition plans. The framework links a least-cost energy-systems
model (\CLEWS{}/\OSeMOSYS{}) with an overlapping-generations general equilibrium
model (\OGCore{}) through a small set of auditable channels: each signal from the
solved energy scenario---the electricity price, grid investment, emissions---is
carried into the economy by exactly one model parameter, and the economy's
equilibrium return and activity are passed back. Both models are open source and
calibrated by countries to their own data, so the same structural mapping is
portable across settings. We illustrate the framework using the Philippines,
coupling the country's own energy-reform scenario to a multi-industry Philippine
calibration of \OGCore{}. The reform raises the levelized cost of electricity by
about $5\%$. Output rises roughly $0.2\%$ over the first decade of the transition,
driven by health co-benefits that raise working-age productivity and save about $95$
lives per year, but settles $0.14\%$ below baseline in the long run, where the
survival gains add retirees and the price channels accumulate. Incidence is
regressive even with full revenue recycling. These results are outputs of the
coupled framework and are not expressible in either model alone. More broadly, the
framework moves the distributional, fiscal, and demographic consequences of energy
policy from assumptions outside the model to outputs of it.
