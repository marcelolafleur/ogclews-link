\section{The Coupling Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

The framework reduces the coupling to two primitive objects and a discipline for
combining them. A \emph{signal} is a quantity carried out of a solved \CLEWS{}
scenario; a \emph{wedge} is a parameter of \OGCore{} that a signal sets; a
\emph{channel} is a named, guard-railed map from a signal to a wedge. The remainder of
this section defines these objects (\Cref{sec:signals-wedges}), identifies the
privileged signal---the dual on the energy-balance constraint
(\Cref{sec:dual})---enumerates the routes by which an energy-cost signal can enter a
core that contains no energy in production (\Cref{sec:routes}), describes the staged
orchestration and the interface contract that make the mapping auditable
(\Cref{sec:architecture}), and states the accounting principles that keep a single
signal from being counted twice (\Cref{sec:discipline}).

\subsection{Signals and wedges}
\label{sec:signals-wedges}

The two models meet across an asymmetry. \OGCore{} is rich in behaviour, public
finance, and household heterogeneity but blind to energy technology; \CLEWS{} is rich
in technology, dispatch, and emissions but blind to behaviour, the fiscal sector, and
distribution. The information that usefully crosses the seam therefore differs by
direction: from the macro side flow \emph{quantities}---sectoral activity and final
demand; from the energy side flow \emph{prices and physical outcomes}---marginal
costs, capital requirements, and emissions. The two primitives formalise the two
directions.

\begin{definition}[Signal]
A \emph{signal} is a scalar or time path extracted from a solved \CLEWS{} scenario:
the dual on a commodity-balance constraint (a marginal cost), the capital investment
required by a build-out, the emissions of a fuel or period, or the dual on an
emissions constraint (an implied carbon price).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Wedge]
A \emph{wedge} is a parameter of \OGCore{} whose perturbation expresses a signal as a
change in household or firm behaviour or in the government budget: the
consumption-tax vector $\tauc$, an industry total-factor-productivity level $\TFP_m$,
the public-investment share $\alphaI$, or the demographic objects---the age-specific
mortality rate $\rho(s,t)$ and effective-labour profile $e(t,s,j)$.
\end{definition}

The term is used in its public-finance sense: the simplest channel
(\Cref{sec:routes}) literally inserts a tax wedge between the producer and consumer
price of energy. A channel is then the composition of an extractor and an applicator,
together with the validation that fires when a mapping is large or ill-posed.

\begin{remark}[Realisation]
The channels are plain functions in \texttt{channels.py} (those that run
\OGCore{}$\to$\CLEWS{} carry an \texttt{emit\_} prefix and execute after the reform
solves); extraction lives in \texttt{signals.py}, application in \texttt{og\_wedge.py},
the per-run interface and sector concordance in \texttt{contract.py}/\texttt{discovery.py},
orchestration in \texttt{framework.py}, and the cross-environment solve---which runs the
country's \OGCore{} model in its own interpreter and exchanges data files---in
\texttt{registry.py}/\texttt{models.py}/\texttt{og\_runner.py}. Everything is
country-agnostic: no hard-coded country, industry index, or industry count are baked in;
those are read from the calibration and the declared concordance (\Cref{sec:architecture}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The dual as the load-bearing signal}
\label{sec:dual}

Among the signals, the shadow price on the energy-balance constraint is privileged.
In the \OSeMOSYS{} program \citep{howells_osemosys_2011} each commodity carries a
balance constraint requiring supply to meet demand in every timeslice and year; the
dual on that constraint is the marginal cost to the least-cost system of delivering
one additional unit of the commodity. Under the usual competitive interpretation it
is the price that would clear that quantity, and it is therefore the natural object to
hand to a demand system. Two properties make it load-bearing.

First, it closes an elasticity gap. Inside the energy optimiser, demand is an
exogenous datum.

\begin{assumption}[Price-inelastic energy demand in \CLEWS{}]
\label{ass:inelastic}
\CLEWS{}/\OSeMOSYS{} solves for the least-cost system that meets a \emph{fixed} energy-
service demand; quantity demanded does not respond to the commodity price within the
optimiser.
\end{assumption}

\noindent The behavioural response must therefore come from the macro side. Feeding
the dual to \OGCore{} households---whose multi-good demand is unit-elastic above a
Stone--Geary subsistence floor \citep{stone_1954}, see \Cref{sec:og}---makes the
quantity of energy demanded respond to its price, supplying the elasticity the
optimiser lacks.

Second, it reveals incidence. A single program-level ``cost'' is silent on who bears
it. \OGCore{}'s households, indexed by $\Ngen$ age and $\Jinc$ lifetime-income groups,
distribute the burden of an energy-price change; because energy is a partial
necessity, the burden is generally regressive---an object a single-agent core cannot
produce.

These two roles define what it means for the coupling to be internally consistent.

\begin{definition}[Soft-link equilibrium]
\label{def:fixedpoint}
Let $D_{\text{OG}}(\pi)$ be the energy demand \OGCore{} implies when households (and,
under \Cref{sec:routes} Route~C, firms) face energy price $\pi$, and let
$\pi_{\text{C}}(D)$ be the \CLEWS{} balance-constraint dual when the system is built to
deliver $D$. A \emph{consistent} coupling is a pair $(\pi^\star, D^\star)$ with
\[
  D^\star = D_{\text{OG}}(\pi^\star)
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  \pi^\star = \pi_{\text{C}}(D^\star).
\]
\end{definition}

\noindent A single \CLEWS{}$\to$\OGCore{} pass evaluates $\pi_{\text{C}}(D^{b})$ at
\emph{baseline} demand and applies it once; iterating the two maps to the fixed point
of \Cref{def:fixedpoint} is \emph{loop closure} (\Cref{sec:architecture}). The
present work validates the single pass; the loop is the principal unbuilt piece.

\subsection{Three routes for an energy-cost shock}
\label{sec:routes}

The shipped \OGCore{} contains no energy as a produced, priced input to firms:
households consume an ``energy good,'' but the firm's technology combines only private
capital, public capital, and labour (\Cref{sec:og}). An energy-cost signal therefore
has no native door into production, and must enter through one of three routes that
trade structural fidelity against the cost of building it.

\paragraph{Route A: a demand-side tax wedge.}
The energy-price signal enters as a consumption-tax wedge on the energy good $i_e$.
Writing $g_t \equiv \pi^{r}_t/\pi^{b}_t$ for the reform-to-baseline energy-price ratio
delivered by \CLEWS{}, the wedge is set so the gross-of-tax price multiplier inherits
that ratio,
\begin{equation}
  1 + \tau^{c,r}_{t,i_e} \;=\; g_t\,\bigl(1 + \tau^{c,b}_{t,i_e}\bigr),
  \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad
  \tau^{c,r}_{t,i_e} \;=\; g_t\bigl(1+\tau^{c,b}_{t,i_e}\bigr) - 1 .
  \label{eq:routeA}
\end{equation}
Households then face $(1+\tauc_{t,i_e})\,p_{i_e,t}$ in the demand system: the quantity
of energy falls and the composite price rises. Route~A reuses existing model
structure and is the only route that delivers heterogeneous incidence directly, but it
carries two liabilities. It taxes only \emph{household} energy and never reaches firm
decisions; and, because $\tauc$ is a fiscal instrument, the mapping mechanically
credits the government with consumption-tax revenue that is economically a resource
cost, not income---``phantom revenue'' that must be neutralised
(\Cref{sec:discipline}). Empirically Route~A is close to output-neutral: the wedge
largely reshuffles consumption rather than shrinking it (\Cref{sec:results}).

\paragraph{Route B: a supply-side productivity haircut, input--output calibrated.}
A cost shock ought to reach firms. Lacking energy in production, Route~B proxies it as
a per-industry productivity loss whose size is fixed by input--output cost
pass-through. Let $\boldsymbol\theta=(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{\Mind})$ be the direct
energy cost shares and $A$ the matrix of interindustry input coefficients. A
proportional energy-price increase $\hat p_E$ raises industry $m$'s unit cost directly
by $\theta_m\hat p_E$ and, through purchased intermediates, indirectly; summing the
Leontief series gives the total cost-push
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf c} \;=\; (I - A^{\!\top})^{-1}\,\boldsymbol\theta\,\hat p_E ,
  \label{eq:routeB}
\end{equation}
which Route~B applies as a haircut $\TFP^{r}_m = \TFP^{b}_m\,(1-\kappa_m)$, with
$\kappa_m$ the gross-output cost increase of industry $m$. The amplification
$(I-A^{\!\top})^{-1}$ is a moderate multiple of the direct shares, calibrated per
aggregation from the country's SAM (\Cref{sec:calibration}).
Route~B gives the right sign---a cost increase contracts output---and, unlike
Route~A, is materially contractionary (\Cref{sec:results}). But a productivity loss is
not an input cost: firms do not substitute away from energy, no energy market clears,
and mapping a gross-output cost onto value-added productivity makes the magnitude a
conservative proxy rather than an exact accounting of the shock.

\begin{remark}[The route is a first-order modelling choice]
\label{rem:ab-contrast}
Routes~A and~B are two representations of the \emph{same} physical energy-cost signal,
yet they deliver opposite macro verdicts---approximately neutral versus sharply
contractionary (\Cref{sec:results}). The divergence is not a calibration detail: it is
the choice of door. This is precisely why a structurally faithful, dual-consistent
representation is the target rather than a refinement.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Route C: energy as a priced production input (the structural ideal).}
The faithful representation nests energy with value added in the firm's technology,
\begin{equation}
  Y^{\text{gross}}_m
  \;=\; \TFP_m\Bigl[\,\omega_m\, VA_m^{\frac{\sigma_{E,m}-1}{\sigma_{E,m}}}
        + (1-\omega_m)\, E_m^{\frac{\sigma_{E,m}-1}{\sigma_{E,m}}}\Bigr]
        ^{\frac{\sigma_{E,m}}{\sigma_{E,m}-1}},
  \label{eq:routeC}
\end{equation}
where $VA_m$ is the existing capital--labour aggregate and $E_m$ is energy purchased
from the energy industry $m_e$ at the dual price $p_{m_e}$. Cost minimisation yields
the energy demand
\begin{equation}
  E_m \;=\; \theta_m\Bigl(\frac{p_{m_e}}{MC_m}\Bigr)^{-\sigma_{E,m}} Y^{\text{gross}}_m ,
  \label{eq:routeC-foc}
\end{equation}
the energy industry clears a resource constraint
$Y_{m_e} = (\text{final demand}) + \sum_{m\ne m_e} E_m$, output is measured as value
added net of energy purchases, and the energy price feeds firm cost endogenously so
that the dual of \Cref{def:fixedpoint} closes the loop on quantities as well as
prices. The substitution elasticities $\sigma_{E,m}\in[0.3,0.8]$ are available from the
energy-CGE literature and the cost shares $\theta_m$ are already calibrated from the
Philippine social accounting matrix (\Cref{sec:calibration}); what Route~C requires is
a production-side extension to \OGCore{} itself, which is out of scope here.

\begin{remark}[A maturity ladder]
The three routes form a ladder: Route~A is cheapest and weakest (existing structure,
household-only, phantom revenue); Route~B is right-signed with a proxy magnitude;
Route~C is structurally correct and dual-consistent but unbuilt. The channels of
\Cref{sec:channels} sit on rungs~A--B, with~C as the stated destination---the same
ladder organises the maturity assessment of \Cref{sec:discussion}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Architecture: a staged soft link}
\label{sec:architecture}

A simultaneous (``hard'') link is ruled out on structural, not practical, grounds.

\begin{assumption}[Incompatible solution concepts]
\label{ass:hardlink}
\OGCore{} solves an overlapping-generations transition path period by period under
foresight; \OSeMOSYS{} is a single perfect-foresight linear program over all years at
once. \OGCore{} exposes no scalar welfare into which the program could be folded, and
no shock can be injected mid-solve.
\end{assumption}

\noindent The models therefore stay separate and exchange a few signals---a soft link,
in the tradition of iteratively coupled energy--macro models
\citep{messner_schrattenholzer_2000,manne_merge_1995,loulou_times_2008}, but with a
behaviourally and fiscally rich overlapping-generations core in place of a
representative agent. The orchestration (\Cref{fig:architecture}) runs in stages:
(i)~solve the \OGCore{} baseline in steady state and along the transition;
(ii)~solve the \CLEWS{} scenario and extract its signals; (iii)~apply the channel
wedges to the reform parameters and solve the reform transition; (iv)~read the
\OGCore{}$\to$\CLEWS{} signals (the equilibrium return $\rp$ and sectoral activity);
and, in the closed loop, (v)~re-solve \CLEWS{} and repeat to the fixed point of
\Cref{def:fixedpoint}.

\diagramfig{architecture}{The structural seam. The two models stay separate and pass
signals across a contract; a hard link is precluded by \Cref{ass:hardlink}.}{fig:architecture}

What makes the exchange auditable is an explicit interface contract
(\texttt{contract.py}): a \emph{scenario pair} naming the baseline and reform runs, the
\CLEWS{} scenario, and the country; a \emph{concordance} mapping \OGCore{} industries
$m$ and goods $i$ to \CLEWS{} commodities, technologies, and demands---supplied by the
country configuration, since \OGCore{} has no native notion of a resource; and a
\emph{unit map} (real versus nominal, base year, deflator) that bridges \CLEWS{}'s bare
numbers to \OGCore{}'s real numéraire. The contract is what lets the same machinery be
re-pointed at another country without touching the channels.

Loop closure is constrained by \Cref{ass:inelastic}. Because energy demand is
price-inelastic inside the optimiser, naïve price feedback can oscillate; convergence
to \Cref{def:fixedpoint} requires damping and a baseline-anchored demand calibration.
The \OGCore{}$\to$\CLEWS{} emitters are built and produce well-formed \CLEWS{} inputs;
what is not yet wired is the outer loop that re-solves \CLEWS{} on them and repeats. Until
it runs, the two \OGCore{}$\to$\CLEWS{} channels (\Cref{sec:ch-discount,sec:ch-demand}) emit
well-formed signals but have no feedback effect on the macro economy.

\subsection{No-double-counting discipline}
\label{sec:discipline}

Because several signals can be expressed through more than one wedge---and because a
fiscal instrument used as a price wedge also moves the budget---the framework needs
explicit accounting rules. We state them as principles; each is enforced by a guard in
the corresponding channel.

\begin{principle}[One carbon price]
A carbon price enters the system once: as an emissions penalty in \CLEWS{} and as a
tax wedge in \OGCore{} along the same path. It is never \emph{also} inferred a second
time from a \CLEWS{} shadow price.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[One view of an energy-cost shock]
The routes of \Cref{sec:routes} are alternative representations of one cost. A run
applies at most one of them to a given cost: a $\tauc$ price wedge, a carbon $\tauc$,
and a $\TFP$ haircut are never stacked on the same energy cost.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[One view of generation capital]
The capital cost of a generation build-out is represented once---through a cost-push
($\TFP$) \emph{or} an investment incentive (an ITC), not both. The capital-share lever
($\gammam$) is \emph{not} a third interchangeable view: in the Cobb--Douglas core it
does not crowd capital in at all but instead lowers the energy price and sheds capital,
a distinct factor-income object (\Cref{sec:ch-capint,sec:discussion}). Conflating it
with the cost-of-capital representations is itself a double-counting error.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[Revenue must be routed]
A $\tauc$ wedge mechanically generates consumption-tax revenue. That revenue must be
recycled (as transfers or public investment) or explicitly financed (as deficit), or
the run silently embeds an unmodelled fiscal expansion alongside the price shock.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[Public and private capital are distinct]
Public-grid capital (reaching \OGCore{} through the public-investment share $\alphaI$
and the public-capital stock $\Kg$) and private-generation capital (reaching the
firm's own capital) touch different objects. Coupling both is complementary, not double
counting.
\end{principle}
