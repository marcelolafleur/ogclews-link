Each channel as a plain story (left) paired with the model part it touches (right). CLEWS→OG and policy channels change an OG input; OG→CLEWS channels read an OG output — their effect returns only through the loop.

CLEWS → OG

Energy price

the cost of energy reaches households

What happensIn the model
1
The energy system gets more expensive to run.
signalthe marginal price of electricity from CLEWS, vs. baseline.
▼
2
That reaches households as a higher energy bill.
pricea price wedge on the energy good (τc) — the only door, since OG has no energy in production.
⌄The wedge then does three things:
Households use less energy.
demanddemand for energy falls (ci); the cost of living edges up (p̃).
Lower-income families are hit hardest — energy is a necessity, a bigger budget share.
who bears itthe subsistence floor (cmin) makes it regressive; across J income × S age.
The money raised goes to government — kept or returned (the user's choice).
revenuebooks cons_tax_revenue; returned as transfers (αT) it isolates the price effect.
A resource-cost passthrough; the same wedge (τc) priced as policy is the carbon channel.

Investment

paying for the public build-out

⚠Public infrastructure only. Private generation capex — the power plants — reaches the economy through the energy price, the capital intensity channel (next), or the energy-capex tax credit — not this (public) one.
What happensIn the model
1
Building the grid takes a lot of money up front.
capexCLEWS public-infrastructure (grid / T&D) capital cost, as a share of GDP.
▼
2
It's added to the government's public investment.
public capitalraises αI → the public capital stock Kg.
⌄which pulls two ways:
The new infrastructure makes the economy more productive.
outputKg lifts output across every industry (via γg).
But it's paid for by debt or taxes, crowding out other spending.
financingthe net sign depends on how the budget is closed.
Whether the economy comes out ahead depends on how the build-out is financed.
CLEWS → OG

Capital intensity

the clean buildout is capital-heavy

What happensIn the model
1
A clean-energy build (wind, solar, carbon-capture) is equipment-heavy — costly to build, cheap to run.
signalCLEWS reform/base ratio of the power fleet's capital-cost share.
▼
2
More of the industry's cost is equipment, less is fuel and labour.
capital shareraises the energy industry's capital share (γm); labour's share is the residual.
⌄the result is not the obvious one:
Energy gets cheaper to produce, so its price falls.
priceshifting cost toward equipment lowers the energy good's price (≈ −¼, PHL).
Demand barely moves, so the same demand is met more cheaply — using less equipment, not more.
capitalenergy-sector capital falls (≈ −14%, PHL); the interest rate r stays roughly flat.
Income shifts from workers toward equipment-owners.
factor splitlabour share is the residual 1 − γ − γg (hard-floored).
A price / income-split lever — not "capital drawn in." Drawing capital in is the energy-capex tax credit (next), which moves energy capital the opposite way; use one, not both.
policy → OG

Energy capex incentive

a tax credit pulls capital into clean generation

What happensIn the model
1
A tax credit makes it cheaper for the energy industry to fund new equipment.
cost of capitalan investment tax credit lowers the energy industry's cost of capital.
▼
2
Capital is drawn into energy — paid for through the public budget.
capital inenergy-sector capital rises (≈ +5%, PHL); funded by the budget.
The counterpart to capital intensity: that changes the cost share (and makes energy cheaper, using less equipment); this changes the cost of capital (and draws capital in). Opposite directions on energy capital — use one, not both.
policy → both

Carbon price

one price, set once, applied both sides

What happensIn the model
1
A carbon price is set once.
one pricea single price (USD/tCO₂), fed to both models.
⌄it acts on both models:
In the energy system, dirty options cost more — the mix shifts cleaner and emissions fall.
emissions · CLEWSenters as an EmissionsPenalty on the least-cost plan.
For households it raises the energy price — they use less, and it raises revenue (kept or returned).
households · OGthe same τc wedge as energy price; books cons_tax_revenue → αT.
The OG side prices household energy only — economy-wide carbon is the CLEWS side.

What happensIn the model
1
The economy settles on an interest rate — how it values the future.
the ratereads OG's equilibrium return on capital (rp) — an output, not changed.
▼
2
That rate is handed to the energy planner as its discount rate.
discount ratesets CLEWS's DiscountRate.
▼
3
It tilts what gets built — patient favours clean, capital-heavy; impatient favours cheap fossil.
build mixchanges the least-cost weighing of up-front vs long-run cost.
▼

via the loopone-way on its own; macro effect appears through iteration.

Health

cleaner air, through health, into the economy

What happensIn the model
1
Cleaner energy means less air pollution.
pollutionthe change in fine particulates (PM2.5) from CLEWS — not CO₂.
▼
2
Less pollution means better health.
dose–responsescaled by an external Global Burden of Disease (GBD) profile.
⌄health reaches the economy two ways:
Fewer early deaths — but mostly elderly, so output barely moves.
demographicsshifts mortality (ρ) → population recomputed; deaths skew old.
Less illness — healthier workers are more productive: the main gain.
productivityshifts effective labour by age (e).
Dose-response calibrated per country: a 1% power-PM2.5 cut → ~0.08% fewer ambient-pollution deaths (PHL) — power is ~10% of ambient PM2.5 (McDuffie 2021) × a concave exposure-response (GBD/GEMM).
