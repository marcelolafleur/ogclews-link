pricea price wedge on the energy good (τc) — the only door, since OG has no energy in production.
⌄The wedge then does three things:
Households use less energy.
demandtheir demand for energy falls (ci); the overall cost of living edges up (p̃).
Lower-income families are hit hardest — energy is a necessity, a bigger share of their budget.
who bears itthe subsistence floor (cmin) makes it regressive; read across J income × S age households.
The money raised goes to the government — kept, or returned to households (the user's choice).
revenuebooks cons_tax_revenue; returning it as transfers (αT) isolates the pure price effect.
Energy price is a resource-cost passthrough; the same wedge (τc) priced as policy is the carbon channel.
